## The Setup

Argentina's parallel FX market has been one of the clearest stress indicators for international investors since capital controls were reimposed in 2019. The gap between the official rate and the blue (informal) rate tells you, in a single number, how much the market distrusts the official price of the peso.

This week, that gap compressed to roughly 11% — the tightest it's been since the current administration took office.

## What's Driving It

Three factors are converging:

**1. Reserve accumulation.** The BCRA has posted three consecutive weeks of net reserve purchases. Gross reserves now sit at ~USD 28.5B. This isn't transformational — net reserves remain negative when you strip out swaps and encumbered assets — but the direction matters more than the level right now. The market is watching the trend.

**2. Fiscal credibility.** January's primary surplus came in at +0.4% of GDP, continuing the administration's zero-deficit commitment. As long as the fiscal anchor holds, the pressure on the central bank to print pesos (and therefore debase the currency) stays contained.

**3. Seasonal tailwinds.** The agricultural export season is ramping up, bringing natural dollar inflows. This is cyclical, not structural — but it gives the BCRA breathing room through Q2.

## What Could Disrupt It

The main risks to continued convergence:

- **IMF review delays.** The 5th review board meeting is scheduled for April 15. A delay or renegotiation would shake confidence quickly.
- **Political noise around the Omnibus Law 2.0.** If legislative negotiations stall, it signals reform fatigue — and the market prices political will as much as economics.
- **Global rates.** If the Fed holds higher for longer, EM currencies face broad pressure. Argentina is not immune, even with its own idiosyncratic drivers.

## The Bottom Line

An 11% FX gap in Argentina is historically tight. For context, this spread was north of 100% under the previous administration. The compression reflects genuine policy progress — but the structural vulnerabilities (negative net reserves, capital controls still in place, mid-term elections approaching) mean this is not a one-way trade.

Watch the IMF review in April. That's the next real catalyst.
